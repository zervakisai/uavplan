\begin{abstract}
We present UAVBench, an open-source benchmark framework for evaluating UAV
path planning algorithms in dynamic urban environments.  The framework
provides three scenarios derived from OpenStreetMap tiles of Greek urban
areas, coupled with physically motivated dynamic obstacles: cellular-automaton
fire spread, traffic vehicles, and no-fly zone corridors.  A contract-based
architecture enforces 33 invariants across 12 families, guaranteeing
deterministic replay, planner fairness, and mask parity.  We evaluate five
planners spanning three families---static search~(A*), adaptive
search~(periodic replan, aggressive replan, D*~Lite), and
reactive~(APF)---across 450 main episodes and 600 ablation episodes.
Results show that adaptive planners achieve 87.8\% overall success
(95.2\% on feasible episodes) versus 0\% for the static baseline,
with Cliff's delta $|d|=0.88$.  Ablation studies isolating each dynamics
layer reveal fire spread as the dominant obstacle source.  All experiments
are bit-identical under the same seed triple, and the framework, data, and
analysis scripts are publicly available.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
UAV path planning, benchmarking, dynamic environments, fire spread,
reproducibility
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) are increasingly deployed in
disaster response scenarios---wildfire perimeter mapping, flood
rescue assessment, and emergency supply delivery~\cite{Erdelj2017,Yuan2015}.
In these settings, the operating environment is not static:
wildfires advance, roads flood, and manned-aircraft no-fly zones (NFZs)
shift as operations evolve.  Path planning algorithms must therefore
handle \emph{dynamic} obstacles that appear, expand, and relocate
during execution.

Despite extensive research on UAV path planning~\cite{Aggarwal2020,Zhao2020},
reproducible comparison of algorithms under controlled dynamic conditions
remains difficult.  Existing evaluations typically use ad-hoc scenarios,
fixed obstacle configurations, or proprietary simulators, making it hard
to isolate the effect of environmental dynamics on planner
performance~\cite{Henderson2018}.  Furthermore, many studies report only
mean success rates without accounting for episode feasibility, seed
sensitivity, or statistical significance~\cite{Agarwal2021}.

We address these gaps with \textbf{UAVBench}, a benchmark framework with
three design principles:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Deterministic reproducibility.}  Given the same
    $(scenario, planner, seed)$ triple, the framework produces
    bit-identical trajectories, event logs, and metrics.  A single
    root RNG, spawned per episode, drives all stochastic components.

  \item \textbf{Planner fairness.}  All planners receive identical
    observations at each step.  Dynamic obstacles evolve \emph{after}
    the agent moves (not between planning and execution), and
    interdictions are placed on the A*-reference corridor independent
    of which planner is under evaluation.

  \item \textbf{Rigorous evaluation.}  We adopt the statistical
    protocol of Agarwal et al.~\cite{Agarwal2021}: interquartile mean
    (IQM) with bootstrap confidence intervals, Friedman
    tests~\cite{Demsar2006}, Cliff's delta effect sizes, and
    performance profiles~\cite{Dolan2002}.  Infeasible episodes
    (structurally unsolvable map layouts) are detected at reset and
    excluded transparently.
\end{enumerate}

The framework implements a Gymnasium-compatible~\cite{Brockman2016,Towers2024}
environment with three urban scenarios built from OpenStreetMap
data~\cite{Boeing2017}, each inspired by real Greek disaster events
(2021~Evia wildfires, 2023~Thessaly floods, 2023~Evros megafire).
Five planners from three algorithmic families are evaluated:
A*~\cite{Hart1968}, D*~Lite~\cite{Koenig2002},
APF~\cite{Khatib1986}, and two A*-based replanning variants.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item An open-source benchmark framework with 33 architectural
    contracts ensuring determinism, fairness, and evaluation integrity.
  \item Three OSM-based urban scenarios with physically motivated
    dynamic obstacles (fire CA, traffic, NFZs).
  \item A 450-episode comparative study of five planners with
    rigorous statistical analysis, plus a 600-episode ablation study
    isolating each dynamics layer.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsection{UAV Path Planning}

UAV path planning has been surveyed extensively~\cite{Aggarwal2020,Zhao2020,LaValle2006}.
Classical approaches include graph search (A*~\cite{Hart1968},
D*~Lite~\cite{Koenig2002}), sampling-based methods (RRT, PRM),
and reactive methods such as Artificial Potential Fields
(APF)~\cite{Khatib1986}.  While these algorithms are well-studied
in static environments, their comparative behavior under evolving
obstacles---particularly fire, traffic, and NFZ dynamics---has
received limited systematic evaluation.

\subsection{Benchmarking and Reproducibility}

Henderson et al.~\cite{Henderson2018} demonstrated that deep RL
results are sensitive to hyperparameters, random seeds, and
evaluation methodology.  Agarwal et al.~\cite{Agarwal2021}
proposed IQM with bootstrap CIs as a more robust alternative to
mean-and-standard-deviation reporting.  Dolan and
Mor\'e~\cite{Dolan2002} introduced performance profiles for
benchmarking optimization solvers.  Dem\v{s}ar~\cite{Demsar2006}
formalized nonparametric tests (Friedman, Wilcoxon) for comparing
classifiers across datasets.  UAVBench adopts all four techniques.

OpenAI Gym~\cite{Brockman2016} established the standard
environment interface for RL research.  UAVBench builds on its
successor, Gymnasium~\cite{Towers2024}, providing a
\texttt{step}/\texttt{reset} API compatible with existing RL
workflows.

\subsection{Dynamic Environment Modeling}

Alexandridis et al.~\cite{Alexandridis2008} proposed cellular automata
(CA) for wildfire spread prediction, using land-use-dependent ignition
probabilities and Moore-neighborhood propagation.  UAVBench adapts this
model with isotropic spread, extended burnout for corridor interdiction,
and smoke diffusion as a secondary blocking layer.
Boeing~\cite{Boeing2017} introduced OSMnx for acquiring and analyzing
street networks from OpenStreetMap; we use it to generate rasterized
urban terrain at 500$\times$500 resolution.

\section{System Design}\label{sec:system}

\subsection{Overview}

UAVBench is a Python framework built around a single Gymnasium
environment (\texttt{UrbanEnvV2}) with deterministic dynamics.
A benchmark runner orchestrates episodes: it resets the environment
with a seed, initializes the planner, and iterates the step loop
until termination.  The runner owns the authoritative step counter;
all components (dynamics, logger, renderer) receive it as a parameter.

The architecture follows a layered design:
\emph{(i)}~scenario configs (YAML) define map, dynamics knobs, and
mission parameters;
\emph{(ii)}~the environment manages grid state, collision semantics,
and dynamics stepping;
\emph{(iii)}~planners implement a unified \texttt{PlannerBase} interface
(\texttt{plan()}, \texttt{update()}, \texttt{should\_replan()});
\emph{(iv)}~the runner enforces the step protocol and collects metrics.

\subsection{Environment}

The environment operates on a 500$\times$500 grid with
$\text{Discrete}(5)$ actions: four cardinal moves and STAY.
The observation includes agent position $(x,y)$, goal position,
and terrain heightmap.  Each cell is either traversable (height~0)
or blocked (buildings, from OSM data).

\textbf{Coordinate convention.}  Public APIs use $(x,y)$ tuples;
internal arrays are indexed as \texttt{array[y,x]} (row-major).
Path entries are \texttt{list[tuple[int,int]]} in $(x,y)$ order.

\textbf{Step semantics.}  Within each step: (1)~the planner
receives the \emph{current} blocking mask (frozen); (2)~it returns
an action; (3)~the environment validates the action against the
current mask; (4)~the agent moves (if valid); (5)~dynamics advance
(fire CA, traffic, NFZ); (6)~a new blocking mask is computed.
This ordering ensures planners see true state---no stale-by-one-step
artifacts.

\textbf{Collision semantics.}  Static obstacles (buildings, permanent
NFZs) cause hard collisions (episode termination).  Dynamic obstacles
(fire, traffic, smoke) cause soft rejections: the move is blocked,
the agent stays in place, and a \texttt{RejectReason} enum is logged.

\subsection{Dynamic Obstacles}\label{sec:dynamics}

Three dynamics layers operate simultaneously:

\textbf{Fire CA.}  Wildfire spread follows a cellular automaton on the
Moore neighborhood (8-connected).  Cell states transition
$\text{UNBURNED} \to \text{BURNING} \to \text{BURNED\_OUT}$.
Spread probability is land-use dependent: urban $p=0.06$, forest
$p=0.15$, road $p=0.03$ (50\% firebreak).  Fire buffer (binary
dilation, radius~1) blocks UAV movement within one cell of active
fire.  Smoke is computed via box-blur diffusion; cells with
smoke~$\geq 0.5$ are blocked.

For corridor interdiction, fire is ignited on the A*-reference path
using approach ignitions (8--15 Manhattan distance) and a safety-net
guarantee: if natural spread has not reached the corridor by the
guarantee step, cells are force-ignited.  Extended burnout
($\tau = 999{,}999$ steps) ensures corridor fires persist for the
entire episode.

\textbf{Traffic.}  $N$ vehicles patrol road cells using greedy
pathfinding.  Corridor vehicles (2 of $N$) patrol near the reference
corridor midpoint.  Vehicle occupancy blocks UAV movement within a
Manhattan buffer (radius~5).  For the flood-rescue scenario,
additional roadblock vehicles are frozen at corridor chokepoints.

\textbf{Dynamic NFZs.}  Restriction zones model manned-aircraft
corridors.  They activate at configurable timesteps and block
rectangular regions.

\subsection{Unified Blocking Mask}

A single \texttt{compute\_blocking\_mask()} function merges all
blocking layers into one boolean grid, used by \emph{both} the
environment step validation and the guardrail feasibility check.
This eliminates mask-parity bugs where the planner and environment
disagree on which cells are blocked.

\subsection{Planners}\label{sec:planners}

Five planners span three algorithmic families (Table~\ref{tab:planners}).

\begin{table}[htbp]
\centering
\caption{Planner suite.}
\label{tab:planners}
\footnotesize
\begin{tabular}{llll}
\toprule
Key & Algorithm & Family & Replanning \\
\midrule
\texttt{astar} & A* & Static & Never \\
\texttt{periodic} & A* + replan & Adaptive & Every $N$ steps \\
\texttt{aggressive} & A* + replan & Adaptive & On mask change \\

\texttt{apf} & APF & Reactive & Every step \\
\bottomrule

\textbf{A*}~\cite{Hart1968} performs a single plan at episode start
on the 4-connected grid with Manhattan heuristic and max 80{,}000
node expansions.  It never replans, serving as a static baseline.

\textbf{Periodic Replan} re-invokes A* every $N$ steps on the
current blocking mask, adapting to obstacle changes.

\textbf{Aggressive Replan} re-invokes A* whenever the blocking mask
changes near the current path, providing event-driven adaptation.

\textbf{D*~Lite}~\cite{Koenig2002} uses A* with obstacle-change
detection.  When obstacles invalidate the current path, it replans
from the current position.  This is a simplified variant; the full
incremental algorithm is not used, as expanding-fire scenarios
cause mass cell changes that negate incremental advantages.

\textbf{APF}~\cite{Khatib1986} computes attractive (goal) and
repulsive (obstacles within $d_0=8$ cells) potentials.  Gradient
descent on the combined field yields the next action.  A per-cell
revisit penalty ($+20.0$) mitigates local minima.  APF falls back
to A* when stuck.

\subsection{Contract Architecture}

UAVBench enforces 33 contracts across 12 families:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{DC} (Determinism): single RNG source, bit-identical replay.
  \item \textbf{FC} (Fairness): corridor-aligned interdictions,
    identical observations for all planners.
  \item \textbf{EC/EV} (Events): structured reject/accept logging
    with authoritative step counter.
  \item \textbf{MP} (Mask Parity): one blocking mask function
    used everywhere.
  \item \textbf{FD} (Fire Dynamics): isotropic spread, no wind,
    deterministic CA.
  \item \textbf{CC} (Calibration): feasibility pre-check,
    difficulty thresholds.
  \item \textbf{SC} (Sanity): adaptive $>$ static in dynamic scenarios.
\end{itemize}

Each contract has an associated test in the \texttt{pytest} suite.
The full specification covers 33 contracts; see the supplementary
material for details.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Protocol}

Each planner is evaluated on all three scenarios with 30 seeds
(seed base~42), yielding $5 \times 3 \times 30 = 450$ episodes.
Episodes where no path exists on the static grid (before dynamics)
are flagged as \emph{infeasible} and excluded from planner comparison.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}

To isolate the contribution of each dynamics layer, we run
$5 \times 3 \times 4 \times 10 = 600$ ablation episodes with
four variants: \texttt{fire\_only}, \texttt{traffic\_only},
\texttt{nfz\_only}, and \texttt{all\_dynamics} (10 seeds each).

\subsection{Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Success rate} (SR): fraction of feasible episodes
    where the agent reaches the goal within the horizon.
  \item \textbf{Path length}: number of cells traversed (successful
    episodes only).
  \item \textbf{Executed steps}: total timesteps including
    rejections and STAY.
  \item \textbf{Replans}: number of path recomputations.
  \item \textbf{Computation time}: wall-clock time per episode.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Statistical Methods}

Following Agarwal et al.~\cite{Agarwal2021}, we report IQM with
95\% bootstrap CIs (10{,}000 resamples).  Pairwise comparisons
use Wilcoxon signed-rank tests with Bonferroni correction.
Cliff's delta quantifies effect sizes.  Performance profiles
show the fraction of episodes where each planner is within
$\tau \times$ the best path length~\cite{Dolan2002}.

Periodic Replan, Aggressive Replan, and D*~Lite produce identical

\textbf{Replan efficiency.}  D*~Lite requires the fewest replans

when the current path is actually blocked.  Aggressive Replan
triggers on any mask change near the path, leading to higher replan
counts without success-rate improvement.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

\textbf{Effect sizes.}  Cliff's delta between A* and each adaptive
planner is $|d| = 0.88$ (large effect).  Between adaptive planners,
$d \approx 0$ (no practical difference in success rate).

\textbf{Significance.}  Wilcoxon signed-rank tests on path length
show APF is significantly different from all other planners
($p < 0.001$, Bonferroni corrected).  The three A*-based adaptive
planners are statistically indistinguishable on path length ($p = \text{NaN}$,
identical distributions).

\textbf{IQM.}  The IQM success rate with 95\% bootstrap CI is
100\% [100, 100] for all adaptive planners and 0\% [0, 0] for A*,
reflecting the bimodal nature of success (either the planner
adapts or it does not).

90\% (fire only) and 97\% (traffic only) to 83\% under all
dynamics, suggesting that the gradient-descent approach is more
sensitive to the combinatorial blocking created by simultaneous
fire and traffic.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{The replanning gap.}  The central finding is a binary
division: planners that replan succeed; the static baseline fails
entirely.  This is by design---corridor interdictions ensure that
the initial A* path becomes infeasible, forcing adaptation.  The
interesting finding is that \emph{how} a planner replans matters
less than \emph{whether} it does.  Periodic, Aggressive, and D*~Lite
achieve identical success rates and path lengths despite very
different replanning strategies (time-triggered, event-triggered,
and path-blocked-triggered).

\textbf{D*~Lite replan efficiency.}  D*~Lite achieves the same
success rate with fewer replans (22 vs.\ 46--95) because it only
replans when the current path is actually obstructed, not when
nearby cells change.  In expanding-fire scenarios, this advantage
is partially offset by the mass cell changes that force full
replanning anyway.  This aligns with Koenig and
Likhachev's~\cite{Koenig2002} observation that incremental search
is most effective when changes are local.

\textbf{APF limitations.}  APF's reactive nature (gradient descent
per step) avoids explicit replanning but produces suboptimal paths
(51\% longer).  Under combined dynamics, its success rate drops
further (83\% vs.\ 97\% for graph-based planners), likely because
simultaneous fire and traffic create complex potential landscapes
with more local minima.

\textbf{Infeasible episodes.}  Dense urban maps (Downtown, 50\%
buildings) produce 13\% structurally infeasible episodes where
no path exists even on the static grid.  Transparent reporting of
infeasibility rates, as recommended by the calibration
contract~(CC-4), prevents these from inflating failure rates
attributed to planner performance.

\textbf{Building density and feasibility.}  The three scenarios
span a range of building densities (0.18--0.50), and we observe
a clear correlation: Penteli (0.18) has 0\% infeasible episodes
and 100\% adaptive success, while Downtown (0.50) has 13\%
infeasible episodes and only 77\% adaptive success.  Dense urban
grids reduce the number of alternative routes, making it harder
for adaptive planners to find detours when the primary corridor
is blocked.  This suggests that building density is a key
difficulty parameter for future scenario design.

\textbf{Limitations and future work.}  UAVBench currently operates
on 2D grids without altitude.  The fire CA is a simplified model
(isotropic, no wind, no terrain slope) that does not capture all
real wildfire dynamics~\cite{Alexandridis2008}.  The five planners
represent classical approaches; learning-based planners (RL,
imitation learning) are a natural extension, as the framework's
Gymnasium interface~\cite{Brockman2016} enables direct integration.
Additional future directions include: \emph{(i)}~altitude as an
action dimension (2.5D planning); \emph{(ii)}~multi-agent
coordination; \emph{(iii)}~wind-driven fire spread for harder
scenarios; and \emph{(iv)}~real-time rendering for
human-in-the-loop studies.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

UAVBench provides a reproducible, contract-verified benchmark for
UAV path planning in dynamic urban environments.  Our evaluation
of five planners across three OSM-based scenarios demonstrates
that replanning capability is necessary and sufficient for
navigating fire-affected terrain, while the specific replanning
strategy has limited impact on success rate.  The framework's
deterministic design, statistical rigor, and open-source release
lower the barrier for fair comparison of future planning
algorithms---including learning-based approaches---in realistic
disaster-response settings.
