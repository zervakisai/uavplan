\begin{abstract}
We present UAVBench, an open-source benchmark framework for evaluating UAV
path planning algorithms in dynamic urban environments.  The framework
provides three scenarios derived from OpenStreetMap tiles of Greek urban
areas, coupled with physically motivated dynamic obstacles: wind-driven
cellular-automaton fire spread (Alexandridis et al.\ 2008), traffic
vehicles, and no-fly zone corridors.  A novel \emph{risk cost map}
translates proximity to hazards into continuous planner costs, enabling
per-planner risk coefficients that differentiate conservative from
aggressive strategies.  Partial observability is modeled via a
\emph{fog-of-war} layer with stale-memory semantics, and a
\emph{triage mission} couples multi-casualty survival decay with
fire proximity.  A contract-based architecture enforces 36 invariants
across 15 families---including wind determinism, fog consistency, and
triage correctness---guaranteeing deterministic replay, planner fairness,
and mask parity.  We evaluate five planners spanning three
families---static (A*), adaptive (Periodic Replan, Aggressive Replan,
Incremental~A*), and reactive (APF)---across 450 episodes with 30 seeds.
On feasible episodes, Incremental~A* achieves 98.8\% success
(Cliff's $|d|{=}0.91$ vs.\ A*), while APF trails at 86.7\%.
Ablation studies isolating each dynamics layer confirm fire spread as
the dominant obstacle source.  All experiments are bit-identical
under the same seed triple.
\end{abstract}

\begin{IEEEkeywords}
UAV path planning, benchmarking, dynamic environments, fire spread,
fog of war, risk-aware planning, reproducibility
\end{IEEEkeywords}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) are increasingly deployed in
disaster response---wildfire perimeter mapping, flood rescue assessment,
and emergency supply delivery~\cite{Erdelj2017,Yuan2015,Yucesoy2025}.
In these settings, the operating environment is not static:
wildfires advance under wind, roads flood, and manned-aircraft no-fly
zones (NFZs) shift as operations evolve.  Path planning algorithms must
handle \emph{dynamic} obstacles that appear, expand, and relocate
during execution, often under \emph{partial observability} where the
agent's sensor range limits its view of the evolving
hazard~\cite{Moustafa2025,Dradoum2025}.

Despite extensive research on UAV path
planning~\cite{Aggarwal2020,Zhao2020}, reproducible comparison of
algorithms under controlled dynamic conditions remains
difficult.  Existing evaluations typically use ad-hoc scenarios, fixed
obstacle configurations, or proprietary simulators, making it hard to
isolate the effect of environmental dynamics on planner
performance~\cite{Henderson2018}.  Furthermore, many studies report only
mean success rates without accounting for episode feasibility, seed
sensitivity, or statistical significance~\cite{Agarwal2021}.

We address these gaps with \textbf{UAVBench}, a benchmark framework with
four design principles:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Deterministic reproducibility.}  Given the same
    $(scenario, planner, seed)$ triple, the framework produces
    bit-identical trajectories, event logs, and metrics.  A single
    root RNG, spawned per episode, drives all stochastic components.

  \item \textbf{Planner fairness.}  All planners receive identical
    observations at each step.  Dynamic obstacles evolve \emph{after}
    the agent moves, and interdictions are placed on the A*-reference
    corridor independent of which planner is under evaluation.

  \item \textbf{Hazard-coupled planning.}  Wind-driven
    fire~\cite{Alexandridis2008}, continuous risk cost maps, fog of war,
    and triage missions create a rich hazard landscape that
    differentiates planners by their risk tolerance, not just their
    replanning frequency.

  \item \textbf{Rigorous evaluation.}  We adopt the statistical
    protocol of Agarwal et al.~\cite{Agarwal2021}: interquartile mean
    (IQM) with bootstrap confidence intervals, Friedman
    tests~\cite{Demsar2006}, Cliff's delta effect sizes, and
    performance profiles~\cite{Dolan2002}.
\end{enumerate}

The framework implements a Gymnasium-compatible~\cite{Towers2025}
environment with three urban scenarios built from OpenStreetMap
data~\cite{Boeing2017}, each inspired by real Greek disaster events
(2021~Evia wildfires, 2023~Thessaly floods, 2023~Evros megafire).
Five planners from three algorithmic families are evaluated:
A*~\cite{Hart1968}, Incremental~A*~\cite{Koenig2002},
APF~\cite{Khatib1986}, and two A*-based replanning variants.

\textbf{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item An open-source benchmark framework with 36 architectural
    contracts across 15 families ensuring determinism, fairness,
    and evaluation integrity.
  \item Wind-driven fire spread (Alexandridis et al.~\cite{Alexandridis2008}),
    per-planner risk cost functions, fog of war with stale-memory semantics,
    and a triage mission with survival decay.
  \item Three OSM-based urban scenarios with physically motivated
    dynamic obstacles (fire~CA, traffic, NFZs).
  \item A 450-episode study of five planners with
    rigorous statistical analysis, plus ablation studies
    isolating each dynamics layer.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}\label{sec:related}

\subsection{UAV Path Planning}

UAV path planning has been surveyed
extensively~\cite{Aggarwal2020,Zhao2020,Moustafa2025,Dradoum2025}.
Classical approaches include graph search (A*~\cite{Hart1968},
D*~Lite~\cite{Koenig2002}), sampling-based methods (RRT, PRM),
and reactive methods such as Artificial Potential Fields
(APF)~\cite{Khatib1986}.  Recent surveys~\cite{Moustafa2025}
emphasize the need for benchmarks that evaluate planners under
dynamic, uncertain conditions rather than static obstacle fields.
Dradoum et al.~\cite{Dradoum2025} classify path planning
algorithms into five families and identify hybrid ablation as
a key evaluation methodology---an approach UAVBench adopts.

Risk-aware UAV planning has gained attention~\cite{Zhou2025},
with methods using continuous cost maps derived from population
exposure, terrain, and hazard proximity.  UAVBench implements
per-planner risk coefficients that translate fire/smoke/traffic
proximity into weighted costs, enabling comparison of risk-tolerant
vs.\ risk-averse strategies.

\subsection{Benchmarking and Reproducibility}

Henderson et al.~\cite{Henderson2018} demonstrated that deep RL
results are sensitive to hyperparameters, random seeds, and
evaluation methodology.  Agarwal et al.~\cite{Agarwal2021}
proposed IQM with bootstrap CIs as a more robust alternative to
mean-and-standard-deviation reporting.  Dolan and
Mor\'e~\cite{Dolan2002} introduced performance profiles for
benchmarking optimization solvers.  Dem\v{s}ar~\cite{Demsar2006}
formalized nonparametric tests (Friedman, Wilcoxon) for comparing
classifiers across datasets.  UAVBench adopts all four techniques.

Gymnasium~\cite{Towers2025} established the standard
environment interface for RL research (NeurIPS~2025 Spotlight).
UAVBench builds on this API, providing a
\texttt{step}/\texttt{reset} interface compatible with existing RL
workflows.

\subsection{Dynamic Environment Modeling}

Alexandridis et al.~\cite{Alexandridis2008} proposed cellular automata
(CA) for wildfire spread prediction, using land-use-dependent ignition
probabilities and Moore-neighborhood propagation.  Recent
work~\cite{Giannino2025} confirms that wind speed and terrain slope
are the dominant parameters governing CA fire dynamics.
UAVBench extends the Alexandridis model with wind-directional spread
modulation, extended burnout for corridor interdiction, and smoke
diffusion as a secondary blocking layer.
Boeing~\cite{Boeing2017} introduced OSMnx for acquiring and analyzing
street networks from OpenStreetMap; we use it to generate rasterized
urban terrain at $500 \times 500$ resolution.

UAVs in disaster response have been reviewed by Erdelj
et al.~\cite{Erdelj2017}, Yuan et al.~\cite{Yuan2015}, and
Y\"ucesoy et al.~\cite{Yucesoy2025}, who identify path planning
under dynamic hazards as a key open challenge.

\section{System Design}\label{sec:system}

\subsection{Overview}

UAVBench is a Python framework built around a single Gymnasium
environment (\texttt{UrbanEnvV2}) with deterministic dynamics.
A benchmark runner orchestrates episodes: it resets the environment
with a seed, initializes the planner, and iterates the step loop
until termination.  The runner owns the authoritative step counter;
all components (dynamics, logger, renderer) receive it as a parameter.

The architecture follows a layered design:
\emph{(i)}~scenario configs define map, dynamics knobs, and
mission parameters;
\emph{(ii)}~the environment manages grid state, collision semantics,
and dynamics stepping;
\emph{(iii)}~planners implement a unified \texttt{PlannerBase} interface
(\texttt{plan()}, \texttt{update()}, \texttt{should\_replan()});
\emph{(iv)}~the runner enforces the step protocol and collects metrics.

\subsection{Environment}

The environment operates on a $500 \times 500$ grid with
$\text{Discrete}(5)$ actions: four cardinal moves and STAY.
The observation includes agent position $(x,y)$, goal position,
terrain heightmap, and---when fog of war is enabled---the fog-filtered
snapshot of the dynamic state.

\textbf{Step semantics.}  Within each step: (1)~the planner
receives the \emph{current} blocking mask (frozen); (2)~it returns
an action; (3)~the environment validates the action against the
current mask; (4)~the agent moves (if valid); (5)~dynamics advance
(fire CA, traffic, NFZ); (6)~a new blocking mask is computed.
This ordering ensures planners see true state---no stale-by-one-step
artifacts.

\textbf{Collision semantics.}  Static obstacles (buildings, permanent
NFZs) cause hard collisions.  Dynamic obstacles
(fire, traffic, smoke) cause soft rejections: the move is blocked,
the agent stays in place, and a \texttt{RejectReason} enum is logged.

\subsection{Dynamic Obstacles}\label{sec:dynamics}

Three dynamics layers operate simultaneously:

\textbf{Fire CA.}  Wildfire spread follows a cellular automaton on the
Moore neighborhood (8-connected).  Cell states transition
$\text{UNBURNED} \to \text{BURNING} \to \text{BURNED\_OUT}$.
When wind speed is zero, spread is isotropic with land-use-dependent
probabilities (urban $p{=}0.06$, forest $p{=}0.15$, road $p{=}0.03$).
When wind speed is positive, spread probability is directionally
modulated per Alexandridis et al.~\cite{Alexandridis2008}:
cells downwind have increased spread probability while upwind cells
are suppressed.  Fire buffer (binary dilation, radius~1) blocks
UAV movement within one cell of active fire.  Smoke is computed via
box-blur diffusion; cells with smoke~$\geq 0.5$ are blocked.

For corridor interdiction, fire is ignited on the A*-reference path
with extended burnout ($\tau = 999{,}999$ steps) ensuring corridor
fires persist for the entire episode.
Fig.~\ref{fig:firespread} shows fire evolution on the Penteli scenario.

\textbf{Traffic.}  $N$ vehicles patrol road cells using greedy
pathfinding.  Corridor vehicles patrol near the reference corridor
midpoint.  Vehicle occupancy blocks UAV movement within a Manhattan
buffer.

\textbf{Dynamic NFZs.}  Restriction zones model manned-aircraft
corridors, activating at configurable timesteps and blocking
rectangular regions.

\subsection{Risk Cost Map}\label{sec:riskmap}

A continuous risk cost map $R: \mathbb{Z}^2 \to [0,1]$ quantifies
proximity to hazards.  Each cell aggregates four risk layers via
element-wise max:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:risk}
  R(x,y) {=} \max\!\Bigl(
    \bigl[1 {-} \tfrac{d_f}{r_f}\bigr]^{\!+}\!,\;
    \tfrac{s}{2},\;
    0.6\bigl[1 {-} \tfrac{d_t}{r_t}\bigr]^{\!+}\!,\;
    \mathbb{1}_\text{nfz}
  \Bigr)
\end{equation}
where $d_\text{fire}$ and $d_\text{traf}$ are Euclidean distance
transforms from the fire front and traffic vehicles, $r_f{=}30$
and $r_t{=}10$ are influence radii (cells), $s \in [0,1]$ is smoke
concentration, and $[\cdot]^+{=}\max(\cdot,0)$.  Each planner
modulates its A*-edge cost as $w(e) = 1 + \rho \cdot R(x,y)$
using a planner-specific risk coefficient $\rho$:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{A*}: ignores cost map (static baseline).
  \item \textbf{Periodic Replan}: risk-averse, $\alpha{=}5.0$.
  \item \textbf{Aggressive Replan}: risk-tolerant, $\beta{=}0.5$.
  \item \textbf{Incremental A*}: moderate, $\gamma{=}2.0$.
  \item \textbf{APF}: risk-modulated repulsion, $\delta{=}3.0$.
\end{itemize}

This mechanism differentiates planners not just by replanning strategy
but by hazard sensitivity, enabling comparison of conservative vs.\
aggressive risk profiles.

\subsection{Fog of War}\label{sec:fog}

Partial observability is modeled via a fog-of-war layer.  When enabled,
the agent observes the true dynamic state only within a configurable
sensor radius centered on its current position.  Outside this radius,
the agent retains a \emph{stale memory} of the last-observed state.
All planners receive the same fog-filtered observation at the same
position (contract~FG-1), ensuring fairness under partial observability.

\subsection{Triage Mission}\label{sec:triage}

The triage mission type introduces multi-casualty survival modeling.
Casualties are placed at configurable locations with initial severity
scores that decay over time according to a survival model coupled with
fire proximity.  The UAV must reach casualties and provide service
(hover for a configurable duration) before their survival probability
drops below a threshold.  Fire-spawned casualties may appear dynamically
when fire reaches building cells, creating emergent task allocation
challenges.

\textbf{A*}~\cite{Hart1968} performs a single plan at episode start
on the 4-connected grid with Manhattan heuristic.  It never replans,
serving as a static baseline that ignores the risk cost map.

\textbf{Periodic Replan} re-invokes A* every $N$ steps on the
current blocking mask with risk-averse cost weighting ($\alpha{=}5.0$).

\textbf{Aggressive Replan} re-invokes A* whenever the blocking mask
changes near the current path, with risk-tolerant weighting ($\beta{=}0.5$).

\textbf{Incremental A*}~\cite{Koenig2002} uses A* with obstacle-change
detection.  When obstacles invalidate the current path, it replans
from the current position with moderate risk weighting ($\gamma{=}2.0$).

\textbf{APF}~\cite{Khatib1986} computes attractive (goal) and
repulsive (obstacles within $d_0{=}8$ cells) potentials with
risk-modulated repulsion ($\delta{=}3.0$).  When wind is active,
repulsive gradients are biased toward the upwind direction, and a
momentum term ($\alpha_m{=}0.7$) smooths oscillation.

\subsection{Contract Architecture}

UAVBench enforces 36 contracts across 15 families:
\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{DC} (Determinism): single RNG source, bit-identical replay.
  \item \textbf{FC} (Fairness): corridor-aligned interdictions,
    identical observations.
  \item \textbf{EC/EV} (Events): structured reject/accept logging
    with authoritative step counter.
  \item \textbf{MP} (Mask Parity): one blocking mask function
    used everywhere.
  \item \textbf{FD/WD} (Fire/Wind): directional spread, wind determinism.
  \item \textbf{FG} (Fog): consistent fog-filtered observations.
  \item \textbf{TR} (Triage): survival decay correctness.
  \item \textbf{CC} (Calibration): feasibility pre-check,
    difficulty thresholds.
  \item \textbf{SC} (Sanity): adaptive $>$ static in dynamic scenarios.
  \item \textbf{PC} (Planner): legal motion, path-progress tracking.
  \item \textbf{GC} (Guardrail): multi-depth relaxation for infeasible episodes.
  \item \textbf{VC/MC} (Visual/Mission): planned-path rendering, HUD, mission story.
\end{itemize}

Each contract has an associated test in the \texttt{pytest} suite
(182~tests total, zero failures).  Table~\ref{tab:contracts}
summarizes the contract families.

\section{Experimental Setup}\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Protocol}

Each planner is evaluated on all three scenarios with 30 seeds,
yielding $5 \times 3 \times 30 = 450$ episodes.
Episodes where no path exists on the static grid (before dynamics)
are flagged as \emph{infeasible} and excluded from planner comparison.
Infeasible episodes are uniform across planners (7 per planner,
7.8\% exclusion rate), confirming fairness contract~FC-2.

\subsection{Ablation Studies}

Three ablation studies isolate individual design factors:
\emph{(i)}~dynamics isolation (fire/traffic/NFZ/all, 600~episodes),
\emph{(ii)}~replan frequency sweep (cadences 3--48, 150~episodes),
\emph{(iii)}~fire intensity sweep (1--8~ignition points, 250~episodes).

\subsection{Metrics}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Success rate} (SR): fraction of feasible episodes
    where the agent reaches the goal within the horizon.
  \item \textbf{Path length}: cells traversed (successful episodes only).
  \item \textbf{Executed steps}: total timesteps including rejections.
  \item \textbf{Replans}: number of path recomputations.
  \item \textbf{Computation time}: wall-clock time per episode.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Statistical Methods}

Following Agarwal et al.~\cite{Agarwal2021}, we report IQM with
95\% bootstrap CIs (10{,}000 resamples).  Pairwise comparisons
use Wilcoxon signed-rank tests with Bonferroni correction.
Cliff's delta quantifies effect sizes.  Performance profiles
show the fraction of episodes where each planner is within
$\tau \times$ the best path length~\cite{Dolan2002}.

\textbf{APF limitations.}  APF achieves the lowest adaptive success
rate (86.7\% feasible) and produces paths 37\% longer than
graph-based planners (515 vs.\ 376 cells, $p < 0.001$ Wilcoxon).
Its reactive gradient-descent approach creates suboptimal paths
and is more susceptible to local minima under combined dynamics.

\textbf{Computation breakdown.}  For Incremental~A* (mean 34s per
episode), approximately 60\% is spent in \texttt{env.step()}
(blocking mask computation and dynamics stepping on the
$500{\times}500$ grid), 25\% in A* pathfinding
(2.6~replans $\times$ up to 80{,}000 node expansions),
and 15\% in metrics and overhead.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

\textbf{Effect sizes.}  Cliff's delta between A* and each adaptive
planner ranges from $|d| = 0.80$ (APF) to $|d| = 0.91$
(Incremental~A*), all classified as large effects.
Between adaptive planners, Incremental~A* vs.\ APF yields
$d = +0.11$ (small), while graph-based adaptive planners are
negligible ($|d| < 0.03$).

\textbf{Significance.}  The Friedman test on success rate across
all (scenario, seed) pairs is significant.  Wilcoxon pairwise
post-hoc tests (Bonferroni corrected) show:
A* vs.\ all adaptive planners $p < 0.001$;
Incremental~A* vs.\ APF $p = 0.016$;
no significant difference among the three graph-based adaptive
planners ($p > 0.11$).

\textbf{IQM.}  The IQM success rate with 95\% bootstrap CI is
100\% [100, 100] for all graph-based adaptive planners,
100\% [71.1, 100] for APF, and 0\% [0, 0] for A*.
The wider CI for APF reflects its higher variance across
scenarios.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\textbf{The replanning gap.}  The central finding is a binary
division: planners that replan succeed; the static baseline fails
entirely.  The dynamics isolation ablation confirms fire as the
primary cause (A* 0\% vs.\ adaptive 97\%), while NFZs alone
leave all planners at 97\%.  The key insight is that
\emph{replan precision} (Incremental~A* with 2.6 replans) outperforms
\emph{replan frequency} (Aggressive Replan with 90 replans).

\textbf{Incremental A* efficiency.}  Incremental~A* achieves the
highest success rate (98.8\% feasible) with the fewest replans and
lowest computation time.  It replans only when the current path
is actually obstructed, avoiding speculative overhead.  In
expanding-fire scenarios, its advantage is partially offset by mass
cell changes, but the moderate risk coefficient ($\gamma{=}2.0$)
helps it select paths that maintain distance from fire fronts.

\textbf{Risk coefficient differentiation.}  The per-planner risk
coefficients create measurable behavioral differences.  Periodic
Replan ($\alpha{=}5.0$, risk-averse) produces slightly longer but
safer paths, while Aggressive Replan ($\beta{=}0.5$, risk-tolerant)
takes shorter but riskier paths.  APF's high risk modulation
($\delta{=}3.0$) amplifies repulsive potentials near hazards,
contributing to longer detours and local-minima traps.
The fire-intensity ablation quantifies this: APF drops to 90\%
at 4+ ignitions while graph-based planners maintain 100\%,
confirming that potential-field methods are structurally
disadvantaged in multi-front fire environments.

\textbf{Building density as difficulty.}  The three scenarios span
building densities 0.18--0.50, and we observe a clear correlation
with both infeasibility rate (0\%--13\%) and adaptive success
degradation.  Dense urban grids reduce alternative routes, making
it harder for planners to find detours when corridors are blocked.

\textbf{Fog of war and triage.}  The fog-of-war layer and triage
mission are architectural contributions verified by contract tests
(FG-1, TR-1) but are not the focus of the main experimental
comparison.  Their primary purpose is to enable future studies on
partial-observability planning and multi-objective mission execution
within the same benchmark framework.

\textbf{Practical implications.}  For real-world UAV deployment,
our results suggest that simple periodic replanning (every 6 steps)
is sufficient when computation is cheap---success rate is 96.7\%
across all cadences $\leq$24.  However, Incremental~A*'s
demand-driven replanning is preferable under resource constraints:
2.6 vs.\ 47 replans, 34s vs.\ 41s, with higher success.

\textbf{Risk coefficient sensitivity.}  The coefficients
$\alpha{=}5.0$, $\beta{=}0.5$, $\gamma{=}2.0$, $\delta{=}3.0$
were selected to span a wide risk-tolerance spectrum (from
risk-averse to risk-tolerant) rather than to optimize any single
planner's performance.  Future work could tune these via Bayesian
optimization or meta-learning across scenario distributions.

\textbf{Limitations and future work.}  UAVBench operates on 2D
grids without altitude.  The fire CA, while wind-aware, does not
model terrain slope effects~\cite{Giannino2025}.  The five planners
represent classical approaches; learning-based planners are a natural
extension via the Gymnasium interface~\cite{Towers2025}.  Future
directions include: \emph{(i)}~altitude as an action dimension;
\emph{(ii)}~multi-agent coordination;
\emph{(iii)}~energy-constrained planning using the built-in energy
budget system; and \emph{(iv)}~full fog-of-war experimental campaigns.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

UAVBench provides a reproducible, contract-verified benchmark for
UAV path planning in dynamic urban environments.  Our evaluation
of five planners across three OSM-based scenarios demonstrates
that (1)~replanning capability is necessary for navigating
fire-affected terrain, (2)~replan \emph{precision} outperforms
replan \emph{frequency}---Incremental~A* achieves 98.8\% feasible
success with only 2.6 replans per episode, and (3)~per-planner risk
coefficients create measurable behavioral differentiation beyond
replanning strategy alone.  The framework's 36 contracts across
15~families, wind-driven fire model, fog of war, and triage mission
provide a rich evaluation substrate for future planning algorithms.
